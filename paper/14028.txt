                                               ABSTRACT

Fiscal foresight---the phenomenon that legislative and implementation lags ensure that private agents
receive clear signals about the tax rates they face in the future---is intrinsic to the tax policy process.
This paper develops an analytical framework to study the econometric implications of fiscal foresight.
Simple theoretical examples show that foresight produces equilibrium time series with a non-invertible
moving average component, which misaligns the agents' and the econometrician's information sets
in estimated VARs. Economically meaningful shocks to taxes, therefore, cannot be extracted from
statistical innovations in conventional ways. Econometric analyses that fail to align agents' and the
econometrician's information sets can produce distorted inferences about the effects of tax policies.
Because non-invertibility arises as a natural outgrowth of the fact that agents' optimal decisions discount
future tax obligations, it is likely to be endemic to the study of fiscal policy. In light of the implications
of the analytical framework, we evaluate two existing empirical approaches to quantifying the impacts
of fiscal foresight. The paper also offers a formal interpretation of the narrative approach to identifying
fiscal policy.

                                         1. Introduction

   Fiscal policy presents researchers with a unique empirical challenge: how to identify and
quantify the impacts of foreseen ‚Äúshocks‚Äù to taxes. The challenge posed by taxes is unique
because few economic phenomena provide economic agents with such clear signals about
how important margins will change in the future. Intrinsic to the process of changing taxes
are two kinds of lags: the legislative lag between when new tax law is proposed and when
it is passed and the implementation lag between when the legislation is signed into law and
when it actually takes eÔ¨Äect. Estimates of the total lag range from a couple of months to
two years or more, depending on the particular legislation being considered.
   Public Ô¨Ånance economists recognize the possibility of Ô¨Åscal foresight and have accumu-
lated empirical evidence of its importance using a variety of econometric and event-study
techniques.1 Macroeconomists sometimes acknowledge the possibility of Ô¨Åscal foresight in
empirical work and occasionally study it in theoretical models, but the empirics are typically
not grounded in theory. This paper is the Ô¨Årst analytical study of the econometric implica-
tions of Ô¨Åscal foresight. Theory suggests that Ô¨Åscal foresight poses a substantial challenge to
econometric analysis of Ô¨Åscal policy.
  Two lines of attack on Ô¨Åscal foresight appear in the empirical macro literature. The Ô¨Årst
estimates conventional VARs, identiÔ¨Åed in a variety of creative ways to isolate ‚Äúanticipated
taxes,‚Äù and then examines the impacts of Ô¨Åscal foresight ex-post [Sims (1988), Blanchard
and Perotti (2002), Yang (2007b), Mountford and Uhlig (2008)]. A second line rejects VAR

  Fiscal foresight poses a formidable challenge because, as Yang (2005) shows, it generates
an equilibrium with a non-invertible VARMA representation.2 Non-invertibility, in turn,
implies that the fundamental shocks to tax policy cannot be recovered from current and

tax policy process, conventional Ô¨Åscal VARs ascribe to the econometrician an information
set that is strictly smaller than the information set on which agents base their decisions. A
smaller information set can lead the econometrician to label as ‚Äútax shocks‚Äù objects that
are linear combinations of all the exogenous disturbances at various leads and lags. This
mislabeling undermines eÔ¨Äorts to quantify how tax policy changes aÔ¨Äect the macro economy.

all the underlying exogenous shocks.
   Misalignment of agents‚Äô and the econometrician‚Äôs information sets has disturbing implica-

value relations do not hold, even when they hold when information sets are correctly aligned.
In sum, failure to address Ô¨Åscal foresight can seriously distort many of the inferences that
macroeconomists draw from empirical work.

that the approaches appear to yield plausible results.4 DiÔ¨Éculties in handling Ô¨Åscal foresight
may explain why no consensus has emerged about whether foresight matters. Existing
empirical work concludes that an anticipated cut in taxes may have little or no eÔ¨Äect [Poterba
(1988), Blanchard and Perotti (2002), Romer and Romer (2007a)], may be expansionary in
the short run [Mountford and Uhlig (2008)], or may be contractionary in the short run
[Branson, Fraga, and Johnson (1986), House and Shapiro (2006), Mertens and Ravn (2008)].

                           2. Evidence of Fiscal Foresight

  Plenty of reduced-form and anecdotal evidence, mostly in the form of case studies of one
or more tax episodes, suggests that private agents possess and react to expected changes
in taxes. Reactions to tax foresight are most evident when policy changes are phased in,
incorporate sunset provisions, or include a delay between enactment and eÔ¨Äective dates.
   Steigerwald and Stuart (1997) develop a residual-based test statistic, which allows com-
parison of predictions errors for econometric models under diÔ¨Äerent assumptions about the
period of tax foresight. By studying investment behavior around major tax legislation that
altered corporate income tax rates, investment tax credits, or the deductions for deprecia-
tion allowances, Steigerwald and Stuart conclude that Ô¨Årms had 4.5-month foresight for tax
changes in 1954, 1962, and 1981, and 16.5-month foresight for the tax reform of 1986.
   In contrast to Steigerwald-Stuart, Poterba‚Äôs (1988) study of consumption responses to
tax announcements associated with legislation in 1964, 1968, 1975, 1981, and 1986, Ô¨Ånds
little evidence of anticipatory behavior. He dates expectational changes as occurring the
month the legislation passes Congress. Poterba regards his Ô¨Åndings as ‚Äútentative‚Äù because
his event study ‚Äúis likely to have relatively low power against the alternative hypothesis that

consumers gradually revise their expectations of future tax policy and adjust consumption
accordingly‚Äù [p. 416].
  The possibility that Ô¨Åscal foresight can produce a ‚Äúpurely anticipatory recession‚Äù was Ô¨Årst
put forth by Branson, Fraga, and Johnson (1986) in an exploration of President Reagan‚Äôs
tax cuts. The Economic Recovery Tax Act, enacted in March 1981, announced a three-stage
tax cut to be phased in from 1982 to 1984. Branson, Fraga, and Johnson argue that in
an open-economy IS-LM model, anticipated tax cuts can reconcile three salient features of
the U.S. macro experience of the early 1980s: an inverted and steep real yield curve, real
appreciation of the dollar, and a severe recession‚Äîall of which occurred in 1981 and 1982,
before the tax cuts were fully realized.
  The Tax Reform Act of 1986 oÔ¨Äered public Ô¨Ånance economists a rich laboratory in which
to test hypotheses that economic agents may respond to news about tax changes before
the changes are eÔ¨Äective. The legislation, which was enacted in October 1986, repealed the
capital gains exclusion from ordinary income, raising the maximum capital gains tax rate
beginning in 1987 from an eÔ¨Äective rate of 20 percent to 28 percent. During the lag between
enactment and eÔ¨Äective dates, capital gains realizations jumped from $172 billion in 1985
to $328 billion in 1986 in anticipation of the tax hike [Congressional Budget OÔ¨Éce (2002,
Table 1, p. 3)].

   Evidence of the impacts of Ô¨Åscal foresight extends well beyond a single piece of legislation.
Yang (2007b) compares responses to a typical tax innovation from VAR systems with and
without interest rates and prices. She Ô¨Ånds that adding the three-month Treasury bill
rate and the GDP price deÔ¨Çator (or commodity prices) to a VAR substantially reduces
the responses of labor, investment, and output to a tax innovation. Since Ô¨Ånancial markets

interpretation that information contained in Ô¨Ånancial variables reÔ¨Çects agents‚Äô knowledge
of future tax policy changes. Sims (1988) Ô¨Ånds similar results for government spending
multipliers.
   Fiscal foresight may also apply to government spending: anticipated shifts in spending may
trigger changes in economic behavior before the spending shift is realized. Recent eÔ¨Äorts by
Ramey (2007) to reconcile the discrepancies in the eÔ¨Äects of government spending using
the narrative approach based on war dummies and VAR approaches lead her to conclude
that the VAR-based innovations are indeed anticipated.6 Since the war dates are identiÔ¨Åed
based on the timing when the media reported future defense buildups, the news reports
predate the actual rises in defense spending. Ramey Ô¨Ånds that the war dates Granger-cause
defense spending and aggregate government spending, but not vice versa. This supports the
existence of foresight about government spending policy.
   There is a line of work that is often interpreted as providing evidence that consumers
do not adjust savings in response to anticipated changes in taxes and that, therefore, Ô¨Åscal
foresight is unlikely to be important in time series data. The work, which uses natural tax
experiments to test the permanent income hypothesis, Ô¨Ånds that agents respond strongly to
predictable changes in tax liabilities and to current disposable income, a result that is taken
as evidence against the hypothesis.7 Because these papers focus on responses at the time of
or after the implementation of the policy change, they are silent about how behavior changes
during the foresight period. In fact, in a neoclassical growth model, where the permanent
income hypothesis holds, consumption responds both at the time that news about a future
change in capital or labor tax rates arrives and after the implementation of the tax rate
change [see Yang (2005)].

approximation error depends upon several factors‚Äîthe complexity of the tax rule (5), the
conditioning variables of the VAR, the structural parameters, the degree of foresight, and so
forth. We show below how these factors confound the analysis in the present setting.

                         6. Assessing the Ex-Ante Approach

   We share the view of the ex-ante approach that, in the presence of Ô¨Åscal foresight, conven-
tional Ô¨Åscal VARs misalign the information sets of economic agents and the econometrician.
In the context of the model in section 3, conventional VARs estimate systems in current
and past values of capital (or output) and revenues. Fiscal foresight implies that those
systems are not invertible and do not adequately capture the Ô¨Åscal news to which agents
respond. When tax rates are exogenous, as in the simple example, information sets are
correctly aligned by including future tax rates in the VAR. This results in a VAR system
in {kt , œÑt+q } and now the fundamental representation is invertible. The ex-ante approach
essentially applies this principle by seeking instruments for expected future tax obligations.
                    FISCAL FORESIGHT: ANALYTICS AND ECONOMETRICS                            25

   The discussion in section 4 and expression (11) make this interpretation more precise.
Although non-invertibility of the moving average representation implies there is no autore-
gressive representation in which the true Ô¨Åscal news is a function of current and past en-
dogenous variables, there is an autoregressive representation in the Ô¨Åscal news and future
endogenous variables. The ex-ante approach uses forecasts of revenue changes associated
with tax legislation to instrument for the information agents possess about future taxes. To
infer the eÔ¨Äects of anticipated taxes on output, the ex-ante approach regresses output against
forecasted revenue changes, among other variables, and interprets the estimated coeÔ¨Écients
causally. To assess the ex-ante approach, we examine the quality of instruments employed.
  Of course, tax rates are not exogenous. They are the outgrowth of a complex set of eco-
nomic and political decisions. Recognizing the intrinsic endogeneity of tax policy decisions,
Romer and Romer (2007a,b) use a narrative method to compile data series that decompose
the forecasted revenue consequences of federal tax changes into ‚Äúendogenous‚Äù and ‚Äúexoge-
nous‚Äù components. Mertens and Ravn (2008) use the Romers‚Äô compiled data series. They
generalize the Romers‚Äô empirical work and lay out an intricate DSGE model to interpret
their estimates of the impacts of anticipated and unanticipated changes in taxes. Whereas
the Romers Ô¨Ånd only weak evidence that private agents react to anticipated tax changes,
Mertens and Ravn obtain provocative and striking results reminiscient of Branson, Fraga,
and Johnson‚Äôs (1986) argument about the Reagan tax cuts: anticipated tax cuts induce sharp
economic slowdowns during the period of Ô¨Åscal foresight, and may even produce recessions.
  In this section we use a standard real business cycle model with proportional capital and
labor tax rates to simulate equilibrium data, including forecasted revenue changes induced
by anticipated and unanticipated tax disturbances. We then run regressions using simulated
data and compare the estimated eÔ¨Äects of foreseen changes in tax rates to the true eÔ¨Äects of
Ô¨Åscal foresight. Because the simulated data and revenue forecasts are generated by a single
coherent model, if the ex-ante approach is eÔ¨Écacious, the regressions should recover the true
eÔ¨Äects almost exactly.

narrative identiÔ¨Åcation scheme in a formal theoretical model.

                                7. Concluding Remarks

   We have explicitly shown how Ô¨Åscal foresight introduces econometric diÔ¨Éculties that com-
plicate the interpretation of VARs. The crux of the issue is that foresight introduces non-
invertible moving average representations into the linear equilibrium process. Non-invertible
moving average representations imply that the mapping between the true tax news that
agents observe and the ‚Äútax shocks‚Äù that the econometrician identiÔ¨Åes can be vastly dif-
ferent. The models illustrate the kinds of mistaken inferences an econometrician is likely
to draw. Many of the econometric techniques in macroeconomists‚Äô toolboxes‚Äîimpulse re-
sponse functions, variance decompositions, Granger-causality tests, tests of cross-equation
restrictions‚Äîcan be badly distorted by empirical methods that do not adequately estimate
the non-invertible moving average components of equilibrium time series.
  Existing time series work on Ô¨Åscal foresight‚Äîwhat we have termed the ex-post and ex-ante
approaches‚Äîseeks instrumental variables that contain the agents‚Äô information about future
tax changes. Because foresight implies that conventional VARs recover ‚Äútax shocks‚Äù that are
convolutions of all the exogenous disturbances, the VAR innovations employed in the ex-post
analyses are likely to be poor instruments. We have shown that the success of the ex-ante
approach depends critically on how the narrative identiÔ¨Åcation scheme is formalized. We
modeled a class of parametric formalizations and found that a wide variety of conclusions
about the approach‚Äôs eÔ¨Écacy are possible.
  Both approaches also fail to model the information Ô¨Çow associated with Ô¨Åscal news. Ex-
post analysis misspeciÔ¨Åes the tax policy rule, while ex-ante analysis leaves the policy rule
                     FISCAL FORESIGHT: ANALYTICS AND ECONOMETRICS                               34

unspeciÔ¨Åed. In the presence of Ô¨Åscal foresight, neither approach correctly identiÔ¨Åes the
behavior underlying tax policy choices.
   Moving beyond the narrow issue of identifying the impacts of Ô¨Åscal foresight, the ex-
ante approach is likely to be of limited utility even if it successfully identifies the impacts of
exogenous news about future tax changes. Imagine that some non-tax shock raises the market
value of government debt; for example, an open-market sale of bonds. In our formalization
of the narrative method, this would raise expected future tax rates. Because the initial
shock carries with it the expectation of higher taxes, the shock‚Äôs impacts will be diÔ¨Äerent
than if, say, lump-sum transfers adjust to stabilize debt. But the ex-ante approach, by
treating forecasted revenue changes as exogenous regressors, does not model the linkages
between non-tax shocks and subsequent changes in revenues. Even if the approach correctly
identiÔ¨Åes Ô¨Åscal foresight, because it does not identify Ô¨Åscal policy behavior, it does not lead to
a way to integrate Ô¨Åscal policy into the broader research programs being pursued at central
banks and policy institutions that are estimating DSGE models.
  Future research will focus on developing the econometric techniques necessary to handle
the issues implied by the analytical framework established here. In so doing, the research
will inform how to integrate Ô¨Åscal analysis into models designed to address a broad range of
economic questions.
