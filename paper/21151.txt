ABSTRACT
This paper proposes a structural approach to measuring the effects of electoral accountability. We
estimate a political agency model with imperfect information in order to identify and quantify discipline
and selection effects, using data on U.S. governors for 1982-2012. We find that the possibility of
reelection provides a significant incentive for incumbents to exert effort. We also find a selection
effect, although it is weaker in terms of its effect on average governor performance. According to
our model, the widely-used two-term regime improves voter welfare by 4.2% compared to a oneterm regime, and find that a three-term regime may improve voter welfare even further.

Introduction

A key aspect of a well-functioning democracy is the accountability of oÂ¢cials via elections.
Elections may improve outcomes by giving incumbents incentives to exert eÂ¤ort, thus disciplining poor performance (Barro [1973], Ferejohn [1986]). They may serve a selection function by screening out low performers (Banks and Sundaram [1993], Fearon [1999], Smart
and Sturm [2013], Duggan and Martinelli [2015]), but may also lead incumbents to pander
to voters with policies that improve their chances of reelection even if they are not socially
bene cial.1
One may thus ask what are the eÂ¤ects of barring reelection at some point by introducing
limits on the number of terms that a politician may serve. Though term limits may reduce
electoral pandering and prevent politicians from becoming too Â“entrenchedÂ” in oÂ¢ce and thus
unresponsive to voter concerns, they may also reduce the incentives for incumbents to exert
eÂ¤ort. They may imply a loss of the bene ts of the experience gained by veteran lawmakers.
Term limits may also reduce the information that voters have about candidates, negatively
impacting the screening function of elections.2 Separating and quantifying these various
eÂ¤ects provides a signi cant challenge to assessing both the positive and the normative
eÂ¤ects of imposing or changing term limits. Examining the eÂ¤ect of term limits further
addresses the larger issue of electoral accountability in the political agency model. The
wide application of this model in political economy suggests the importance of assessing its
empirical relevance.
Many papers have used a reduced-form approach to try to estimate the eÂ¤ects of term
limits. We discuss these papers in greater detail in Section 2. By its very nature, reducedform estimation faces the diÂ¢culty of disentangling the importance of various factors Â– such
as discipline versus selection Â– on the net eÂ¤ect of term limits. Nor can such an approach be
used to consider counterfactual experiments central to assessing the welfare impact of term

thus electoral accountability. We set out a political agency model with adverse selection and
moral hazard. In the model politicians are of two types: Â“goodÂ”, who are always willing to
exert high eÂ¤ort; and Â“badÂ”, who would exert low eÂ¤ort in the absence of incentives, such
as the possibility of another term in the oÂ¢ce. Neither the eÂ¤ort level chosen by politicians

Literature

As indicated above, there have been a number of papers using reduced-form estimation to
test the eÂ¤ects of term limits on politician performance. For example, Besley and Case

of reelection-eligible governors and lame-duck governors can only reveal a net eÂ¤ect.4

One of the key advantages of our structural approach is the ability to conduct counterfactual
exercises to see how outcomes would diÂ¤er if we changed various aspects of the environment
such that the governors and voters behaved diÂ¤erently than they do in the data. We use
this advantage for two important purposes. First, in this section we measure the magnitudes
of the eÂ¤ects of having the possibility of reelection. Second, in Section 5.5, we consider a
change in term limits.
Elections have three consequences in our model: discipline (bad governors exert high
eÂ¤ort to secure reelection), selection (more good governors are reelected since bad governors
who exerted low eÂ¤ort are identi ed), and mimicking (bad governors who are disciplined look
like good governors). In order to measure the rst two eÂ¤ects, we compare the outcomes in
the benchmark model with a counterfactual model where governors can only serve one term.

Having more disciplined governors increases rst-term outcomes relative to the one-term case

Conclusions

In this paper we constructed a political agency model with adverse selection and moral
hazard, and we structurally estimated the model. The aim was to disentangle the various
eÂ¤ects that electoral accountability has on policymaker performance Â– speci cally discipline
and selection eÂ¤ects Â– and, more generally, to assess the empirical relevance of the widelyused political agency model.
Many papers have used a reduced-form approach to try to estimate the eÂ¤ects of electoral
accountability on discipline and selection, but this approach faces the diÂ¢culty of disentangling the importance of these two eÂ¤ects on policymaker performance. Structural estimation
allows us to separate empirically the discipline and selection eÂ¤ects of elections. We estimated the eÂ¤ects on the performance of U.S. governors of the common two-term limit regime
relative to the counterfactual case where reelection is not allowed, so that elections can neither discipline nor allow selection based on performance. A crucial advantage of a structural
model is the possibility of estimating speci c parameters representing discipline eÂ¤ects and
the relative prevalence of governor types, a possibility that reduced-form estimation does
not allow. This is what allows counterfactual experiments to assess the welfare eÂ¤ects of
electoral accountability under diÂ¤erent informational and electoral regimes.
We found a signi cant discipline eÂ¤ect of reelection incentives, as well as a somewhat
weaker selection eÂ¤ect. Quantifying these eÂ¤ects allows us to assess their relative importance.
More generally, our results indicate that a formal political agency model stressing the role
of accountability nds support in the data, an important point given the widespread use of
the political agency approach in theoretical political economy models.
Further research may help address some basic questions raised by these results. Why is
there such a large fraction of Â“badÂ” governors in the data? Why donÂ’t reelection incentives
discipline a larger fraction of them? Arguing that there is a large stochastic element to
elections doesnÂ’t really answer the second question. These two questions are of course related.
Understanding why some governors donÂ’t perform well should help explain why the threat
of not being reelected may not induce them to perform better.
In our opinion, structural estimation can be quite helpful in gaining a deeper econometric
understanding of issues of politician performance and electoral accountability. We believe
this paper is a useful step in that direction.
