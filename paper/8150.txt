       Convergence in per capita income depends on the degree of international technology diÔ¨Äusion.

Strong diÔ¨Äusion of technological knowledge favors convergence, while the absence of it can lead to

divergence if the domestic rate of technological change varies across countries. A case in point is

the faster implementation of recent advances in information technology in the United States (U.S.)

compared to other countries. This has been cited as major reason of why the U.S.‚Äôs lead in per

capita income over Japan has increased from 10% in 1990 to 20% by 1999 (e.g., McKinsey 2000,

Economist 2000). The scope of technology diÔ¨Äusion also matters for income convergence among the

world‚Äôs advanced (‚ÄùNorth‚Äù) and less developed countries (‚ÄùSouth‚Äù). For instance, the issue is widely

discussed in the context of the ‚Äùdigital divide‚Äù scenario‚Äîthe widespread fear that the internet might

not lead to convergence, but instead to a further polarization of per capita income in the world.

       This paper studies international technology diÔ¨Äusion among the world‚Äôs seven major industrial-

ized countries on a geographic basis. It is well-known, for instance, that foreign direct investment

(FDI) patterns are aÔ¨Äected by spatial factors, and it is a stylized fact that the volume of bilateral

trade declines with distance (e.g., Caves 1996, Leamer and Levinsohn 1995, respectively). Because

trade and FDI patterns might determine a country‚Äôs access to embodied foreign technology in form

of advanced intermediate goods, these mechanisms are both plausible channels of technological dif-

fusion.1 Disembodied technology diÔ¨Äusion in form of direct communication could be another major

way of how technological knowledge moves between countries, and while distance aÔ¨Äects the likeli-

hood of face-to-face interactions, it matters much less for communication via telephone or email.2

Rather, language and other cultural-historic factors play a relatively larger role for communication

√üows than for trade or FDI. At this time however, relatively little is known on how geographic and

other factors impact technology diÔ¨Äusion among countries.

   My empirical analysis will √ûrst address the question whether geographic distance aÔ¨Äects the

degree of diÔ¨Äusion. In particular, do remotely located countries have a smaller stock of technological

knowledge at their disposal than more centrally located countries? Second, I will study whether

this relationship has changed over time. The analysis has major implications for economic policies

towards growth and innovation, because if technological knowledge diÔ¨Äuses fully as well as quickly,

such policies cannot raise a country‚Äôs relative welfare. Third, I will examine whether trade, FDI,

and communication matter as speci√ûc channels of technology diÔ¨Äusion. Going beyond the analysis

of distance is important, because economic policy might be powerful in aÔ¨Äecting trade, FDI, or

communication patterns, whereas it cannot, at least literally, aÔ¨Äect a country‚Äôs geographic location

relative to other countries.

   This paper builds on a substantial amount of work showing that the link between the research

and development (R&D) spending in one industry and productivity in another is best viewed as

a process of technology diÔ¨Äusion (Scherer 1984, Griliches 1995). It is based on data for two- and

three-digit manufacturing industries in Canada, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, the United Kingdom

(U.K.), and the U.S.‚Äîthe so-called G-7 countries‚Äî during the years of 1970 to 1995. The G-7 countries

account for more than 90% of the world‚Äôs R&D spending, and also by most other measures, these

countries are among the technologically most-advanced in the world. I refer thus to the G-7 countries

collectively as the world‚Äôs technology frontier.

   Recent contributions showing that strong technology diÔ¨Äusion favors convergence while divergence

is likely if technological knowledge remains local include Feenstra (1996) and Grossman and Helpman

(1991). In the empirical literature, Eaton and Kortum (1999, 1996) estimate models of technology

diÔ¨Äusion and productivity growth. The Eaton and Kortum (1996) estimates from an equation of

patenting activity suggest that technology diÔ¨Äusion declines with geographic distance, a √ûnding that

is primarily identi√ûed from variation of within- versus across-country patenting. JaÔ¨Äe, Trajtenberg,

4    Summary and discussion

This analysis of technology diÔ¨Äusion among the seven major industrialized countries has produced a

number of interesting results. First, geographic distance appears to have a strongly limiting eÔ¨Äect on

technology diÔ¨Äusion among these technology frontier-countries. While the estimates vary somewhat

depending on speci√ûcation, typically they imply a technology half-life in terms of distance of 800


                                                  25
to 1,900 kilometers. Second, the degree of localization of technology diÔ¨Äusion has substantially

declined over the sample period. Again, estimates vary somewhat, but it appears that the extent of

localization has fallen by at least two thirds from the 1970s to the 1990s. Third, I have presented a

number of √ûndings on the importance of trade, FDI, and language skills for international technology

diÔ¨Äusion, to which I turn below.

   The eÔ¨Äect that distance has on the scope of international technology diÔ¨Äusion according to some

of my estimates is probably too high. One reason for this could be the fact that my analysis abstracts

from the value of technological knowledge being heterogeneous. It is well-known from analyses of

the value of patents that their distribution is very skewed. Because the technology that diÔ¨Äuses

√ûrst is likely more valuable than the technology that diÔ¨Äuses later, my analysis underestimates the

value of small stocks of diÔ¨Äused technology relative to larger stocks. In particular, taking account of

heterogeneity might therefore raise technology diÔ¨Äusion to and from Japan. Caution is also needed to

interpret the results on changes in technology diÔ¨Äusion over time. While there are several mechanisms

which seem to be plausible a priori, the dramatic magnitude that I estimate, often eliminating the

localization eÔ¨Äect completely over only twenty-√ûve years, suggests that it might be overstated.

   As data on a larger set of countries, especially outside Europe, becomes available, it will be

possible to re-examine the questions I have addressed. Moreover, it might be possible in the future

to compute productivity indices that consistently account for diÔ¨Äerences in human capital across

countries and industries. In terms of speci√ûcation, I have focused on international within-industry

eÔ¨Äects, while technology diÔ¨Äusion between industries‚Äîthat is, across technology space‚Äîis likely to be

important as well. Further, the temporal dimension of technology diÔ¨Äusion has been collapsed into

one point in time in my analysis that focuses on contemporaneous eÔ¨Äects.

   For the time being, then, what explains the level and the change in the localization eÔ¨Äect that

are estimated? I have considered the channels of trade, FDI, and direct communication, proxied by


                                                  26
data on language skills, as alternatives to distance above. Recall that the interpretation of these

√ûndings requires caution for the reasons discussed in section 1.3. From this analysis, it appears

that a substantial portion of the distance eÔ¨Äect in technology diÔ¨Äusion, and may be all of it, can be

accounted for by diÔ¨Äerences in trade, FDI, and communication links across countries. To the extent

that this √ûnding is con√ûrmed by future research, this provides important information for areas where

economic policy might be eÔ¨Äective in fostering international technology diÔ¨Äusion. Out of the three

channels, I estimate that trade is most important, with about two-thirds of the total diÔ¨Äusion eÔ¨Äect,

while diÔ¨Äerences in FDI and language skills account for about one-sixth each.35 These √ûndings are

to some extent speci√ûc to this sample of major OECD countries, and it will be interesting to see by

how much the estimates change once the analysis is extended to a broader set of countries.

    While it is possible to account for a substantial part of the distance eÔ¨Äect in terms of trade, FDI,

and communication links, much less can be said at this point on what has caused the decline in the

degree of localization of technology over the sample period. Have transport costs for goods declined

dramatically over the period of 1970-95? Direct evidence on this is scarce. Research in international

trade using so-called gravity equations has frequently shown that the volume of trade falls sharply

with geographic distance, but whether this eÔ¨Äect has become substantially weaker during the sample

period is not settled yet.36 Thus, it cannot be ruled out that less localization of technology diÔ¨Äusion

is related to the higher level of economic integration through trade that has been observed in recent

years. As for foreign direct investment, the rate of growth in multinational activity over the last two

decades has been even higher than the rate of growth of world trade, which means that FDI might

also be in part what is behind the decrease in localization of technology. And of course the recent
