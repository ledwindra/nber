ABSTRACT
Changes in fiscal policy typically entail two kinds of lags: the legislative lagâ€”between when legislation
is proposed and when it is signed into lawâ€”and the implementation lagâ€”from when a new fiscal
law is enacted and when it takes effect. These lags imply that substantial time evolves between when
news arrives about fiscal changes and when the changes actually take placeâ€”time when households
and firms can adjust their behavior. We identify two types of fiscal newsâ€”government spending and
changes in tax policyâ€”and map the news processes into standard DSGE models. We identify news
concerning taxes through the municipal bond market. If asset markets are efficient, the yield spread
between tax-exempt municipal bonds and treasuries should be a function of the news concerning changes
in tax policy. We identify news concerning government spending through the Survey of Professional
Forecasters. We conclude that news concerning fiscal variables is a time-varying process that can have
important qualitative and quantitative effects.

One of the main contributions of the paper is the identiï¬cation of ï¬scal foresight through
various empirical sources. We back out foresight with respect to changes in tax policy via the
municipal bond market. We use data from the Survey of Professional Forecasters and Ramey

3.1 Identification of Tax Foresight If markets are eï¬ƒcient, asset prices reï¬‚ect all
information currently available to market participants, especially news concerning the future
paths of relevant variables. This hypothesis led Beaudry and Portier (2006) to include stock
prices in a VAR in order to capture agentsâ€™ expectations about future changes in productivity,
while Fisher and Peters (2009) use stock prices to identify news about government spending.

There are two dimensions to ï¬scal foresightâ€”horizon and intensity. A foresight horizon
measures how far in advance agents are aware of potential changes to ï¬scal policy. Foresight
intensity measures how conï¬dent agents are about pending changes to ï¬scal variables.
As an example of a foresight horizon, consider changes to the tax code. The foresight

would have placed positive probability on a decrease in tax rates, which would have preceded
the legislative lag by several years.

Perotti (2002), Mountford and Uhlig (2009), Mertens and Ravn (2008, 2009)]. This section
explores, in the context of the two DSGE models, how diï¬€erent assumptions about the degree
of ï¬scal foresight and the nature of the ï¬scal news processes alter the modelsâ€™ predictions of
the impacts of anticipated ï¬scal changes. With the exception of the ï¬scal news processes,
parameters in both models are set at the values estimated or calibrated by Braun (1994),
reported in appendix C.1, and Traum and Yang (2009), reported in appendix C.2.
Results in this section employ the estimated ï¬scal rules in the two models: equations

Figures 6â€“8 report responses of consumption, output, employment, and investment in the
RBC model to changes in labor taxes, capital taxes, and government spending. Solid black

changes in ï¬scal instruments. The other three lines are associated with a high degree of
foresight (heavy dotted-dashed line), a medium degree (light dotted-dashed line), and a
low degree (dashed line). Relative to a surprise tax increase, foresight about higher labor

We now turn to the NK model. One important distinction between the RBC model
and the NK model is that the NK model has many frictions (e.g., investment adjustment
costs, monopolistically competitive intermediate goods and labor sectors, variable capital
utilization, etc.) built into the model in order to provide a tighter ï¬t to data. These
frictions serve to smooth out the response of agents to news about future changes to tax
rates and government spending. Figure 9 shows the response to a capital tax shock to the
NK model. Notice that relative to the RBC model, where most of the diï¬€erence between
foresight and no foresight occurs in the ï¬rst 6 quarters, the diï¬€erent responses of output,
investment, and aggregate consumption to diï¬€erent speciï¬cations of news is nearly negligible
for the ï¬rst year. However, the impulse response functions of the RBC model converge more
quickly than do those of the NK model. In other words, the frictions of the NK model serve
to smooth the initial response of news shocks but also propagate their eï¬€ects.
To better understand how the frictions of the NK model interact with ï¬scal foresight,
we plot impulse response functions with speciï¬c frictions turned oï¬€. Figure 10 plots the
response of investment to a capital tax shock with investment adjustment costs and variable

impulse responses for high foresight and no foresight is much larger when the frictions are
turned oï¬€. The intuition is straightforward: as adjustments to capital become less costly,

In addition to the absence of frictions, the NK model diï¬€ers from the RBC model in
its speciï¬cation of ï¬scal ï¬nancing. In the RBC model, all ï¬scal ï¬nancing operates through
contemporaneous lump-sum taxes. The NK model, in contrast, initially uses debt ï¬nancing
of ï¬scal deï¬cits. Debt ï¬nancing, in turn, portends adjustments in a mix of future distorting
taxes, government spending, government investment, and transfer payments. These adjustments induce additional dynamics that add to the propagation of ï¬scal disturbances in
the NK model relative to the the RBC setup. They also have important consequences for
the impacts of varying degrees of ï¬scal news. Figure 12 shows that the eï¬€ect of government
spending foresight can have large eï¬€ects in the NK model. The black line shows the response
with no foresight to an increase in government consumption. The usual result follows: investment and consumption fall due to the government absorbing a larger share of goods,
while output increases. However with a high degree of foresight, output could fall in period
t as agents anticipate a much higher increase in government consumption in period t + 3.
This suggests that no-foresight impact multipliers would be positive, while the high-foresight
multipliers would be negative.

6 Conclusion
We ï¬nd that news concerning changes in ï¬scal policy is a time-varying process. There
are periods in which agents have many quarters of foresight (e.g., wars, signiï¬cant changes
to the tax code). Over the time horizon that we examine, these â€œhigh foresightâ€ periods
are few and far between. Much of the data consist of medium to low or no foresight.
One consequence of this result is that models that do not take the time varying process of
information ï¬‚ows into account will average away the eï¬€ects of news. These studies might
conclude that ï¬scal foresight is not relevant for explaining business cycle dynamics, but
these models will not be able to assess the eï¬€ects of ï¬scal foresight. We examined ï¬scal
foresight in Braunâ€™s (1994) real business cycle model and Traum and Yangâ€™s (2009) new
Keynesian model. We have shown how foresight interacts with common frictions imbedded in
models to better ï¬t data. Internal propagation mechanisms, such as investment adjustment
costs, are shown to propagate the eï¬€ects of foresight. We ï¬nd that ï¬scal foresight has

news processes substantially alter equilibrium dynamics, underscoring the importance of
accurately characterizing the stochastic processes governing ï¬scal news.
