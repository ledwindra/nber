ABSTRACT
The paper generalizes the Taylor principle---the proposition that central banks can stabilize the
macroeconomy by raising their interest rate instrument more than one-for-one in response to higher
inflation---to an environment in which reaction coefficients in the monetary policy rule evolve
according to a Markov process. We derive a long-run Taylor principle that delivers unique bounded
equilibria in two standard models. Policy can satisfy the Taylor principle in the long run, even while
deviating from it substantially for brief periods or modestly for prolonged periods. Macroeconomic
volatility can be higher in periods when the Taylor principle is not satisfied, not because of
indeterminacy, but because monetary policy amplifies the impacts of fundamental shocks. Regime
change alters the qualitative and quantitative predictions of a conventional new Keynesian model,
yielding fresh interpretations of existing empirical work.

information about the state of the economy.

regime can change; knowing this, private agents will ascribe a probability distribution
to regimes. Expectations formation and, therefore, the resulting equilibria will reÔ¨Çect

4. Some Practical Implications of Regime Switching
We turn now to the implications of monetary policy processes that empirical evidence suggests are relevant. In practice, obeying the Taylor principle is viewed as
desirable because of its well-known stabilization properties and its ability to prevent
Ô¨Çuctuations due to self-fulÔ¨Ålling expectations. However, no central bank systematically implements a policy with the primary goal of satisfying the Taylor principle on
a period-by-period basis. Instead, central banks seem to have internalized Taylor‚Äôs
key prescription: on average, raise nominal interest rates more than one-for-one with
inÔ¨Çation. But central banks also desire the Ô¨Çexibility to respond to developments
that may entail a departure from the Taylor principle. Should such departures be
of concern? Addressing this question requires a complete speciÔ¨Åcation of the monetary policy process‚Äîthe degree of the departure, given by regime-dependent values
of the policy rule coeÔ¨Écients, and the duration of the departure, determined by the
transition probabilities.
Two types of departures from the Taylor principle are of particular interest in
describing Federal Reserve behavior. The Ô¨Årst arises when private agents believe there
is a small probability of returning to a persistent regime like the one that prevailed in
the 1970s. This policy process reÔ¨Çects empirical work that Ô¨Ånds U.S. monetary policy
followed very diÔ¨Äerent rules from 1960 to 1979 and after 1982. The second kind of
departure occurs when central banks abandon their ‚Äúbusiness-as-usual‚Äù rule and do
something diÔ¨Äerent for brief periods of time. Examples include the October 1987 stock
market crash, Asian and Russian Ô¨Ånancial crises in the 1990s, credit controls in 1980,
sluggish job-market recoveries from recessions, and currency crises. These are events
with small probability mass that recur and can entail a substantial deviation from
the usual rule. We model these events as relatively short-lived excursions into passive
policy behavior, though we recognize that this is, at best, a crude representation of
the diversity of examples listed above.
Departures from a constant rule obeying the Taylor principle have two possible
ramiÔ¨Åcations. The Ô¨Årst is determinacy of equilibrium and the second is volatility
arising from expectation formation eÔ¨Äects. We examine these ramiÔ¨Åcations for the
two types of departures from the Taylor principle.

4.1. A Return to the 1970s? Many observers of U.S. monetary policy fear that
the Fed could revert to the policies of the 1970s. Such a fear is often behind arguments for adopting inÔ¨Çation targeting in the United States [Bernanke and Mishkin
(1997), Bernanke, Laubach, Mishkin, and Posen (1999a), Mishkin (2004), Goodfriend
(2005)]. The United States seems particularly susceptible to this kind of policy reversal because, in the absence of institutional reforms, the Fed relies on what Bernanke,
Laubach, Mishkin, and Posen (1999b) call the ‚Äújust trust us‚Äù approach, which relies
more on the personal credibility of policy makers than on the credibility of the policy
institution or the policymaking process.13
Three widely cited empirical studies report constant-coeÔ¨Écient estimates of Taylor rules for the United States [Clarida, Gali, and Gertler (2000), Taylor (1999a),

was passive through the 1960s and 1970s and active since 1982. EÔ¨Äorts to estimate
Markov-switching versions of these rules frequently Ô¨Ånd analogous results [Favero and
Monacelli (2005), Davig and Leeper (2006b)]. A literal interpretation of the switching
results is that agents place substantial probability mass on a return to the inÔ¨Çationary
times of the 1970s.

and Schorfheide infer that their estimates imply a determinate equilibrium. In the
latter period, for the mean of the posterior distribution they estimate Œ±1 = 2.19
and Œ≥1 = .3, while for the earlier period the estimates are Œ±2 = .89 and Œ≥2 = .15.
Their maximum likelihood estimates contrast the Ô¨Åt of determinate to indeterminate
equilibria under the maintained assumption that policy rules cannot change.

5.2. Quantitative Predictions of the Impacts of Shocks. To illustrate the
potential expectations formation eÔ¨Äects from a belief that policy might return to

mean there is a 5 percent chance of returning to a passive policy rule. The active
regime is expected to last 20 quarters, while the passive regime lasts 14 quarters, on
average. We gauge the extent that expectations of a future passive regime aÔ¨Äects the
equilibrium in the active regime by contrasting responses of inÔ¨Çation and output to

Expectations formation eÔ¨Äects from this policy process are substantial. Figure 5

6. Concluding Remarks
This paper oÔ¨Äers a broader perspective on the Taylor principle and the range of
unique bounded equilibria it supports by allowing policy regime to vary over time.
Examples show that endowing conventional models with empirically relevant monetary policy switching processes can generate important expectations formation eÔ¨Äects.
These eÔ¨Äects can alter the qualitative and quantitative predictions of standard models. Along the way, the paper develops a two-step solution method that obtains
determinacy conditions and solutions for a rational expectations equilibrium. This

regimes.
The paper‚Äôs results should be useful for both researchers and policy analysts using
constant-coeÔ¨Écient policy rules in DSGE models. The choice of how to model deviations from such rules is potentially quite important. Under prevailing practice, that
choice is made implicitly. That choice should be explicit, with careful consideration
given to the characteristics of the deviation‚Äîhow likely is it to recur? how long is it
likely to last? what is the nature of policy behavior during the period of deviation?
Some deviations are more naturally modeled as additive, exogenous errors to the
policy rule. Some might be better modeled as systematic responses to an expanded
information set for the policy authority. Others are best treated as recurring changes
in rules mapping endogenous variables to policy choices, as in this paper.
Modeling policy as we do in this paper requires no more heroic assumptions than
those routinely made in policy research. Largely as a matter of convenience, nearly
all theoretical models assume‚Äîrather heroically‚Äîthat future policy is current policy. When the current regime is an absorbing state, this assumption is reasonable.
If, as seems more likely, alternative future policies are possible, then rational agents
must have a probability distribution over those policies, and the properties of observed equilibria will depend critically agents‚Äô beliefs about those policies and their
probabilities.
