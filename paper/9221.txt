ABSTRACT
This paper presents a fiscal theory of sovereign risk and default. Under certain monetaryfiscal regimes, the risk of default, and thus the emergence of sovereign risk premia, are inevitable.
The paper characterizes the equilibrium processes of the sovereign risk premium and the default rate
under a number of alternative monetary policy arrangements. Under some of the policy
environments considered, the expected default rate and the sovereign risk premium are zero although
the government defaults regularly. Under other monetary regimes the default rate and the sovereign
risk premium are serially correlated and therefore forecastable. Environments are characterized
under which delaying default is counterproductive.

Introduction

Certain monetary-ï¬scal arrangements are incompatible with price stability and government
solvency. Consider, for example, the case of an independent central bank whose policy is to
peg the price level. Under this monetary regime, the government cannot use the price level
as a shock absorber of negative ï¬scal shocks. Speciï¬cally, by sticking to a price level target,
the government gives up its ability to inï¬‚ate away part of the real value of public debt via
surprise inï¬‚ation in response to a sudden deterioration of the ï¬scal budget. Under these
circumstances, default of the public debt is inevitable.1
Policy regimes of this type, under which debt repudiation is, under certain states of the
world the only possible outcome, are not unheard of. A point in case is the recent debt
crisis in Argentina. Between 1991 and early 2002, Argentina pegged the domestic price of
tradables to the US counterpart by ï¬xing the peso/dollar exchange rate. Abandoning the
exchange-rate peg was never an easy option for the Argentine government. This is because
the peg was instituted by a law of Congressâ€”the 1991 Convertibility Lawâ€”which required
the enactment of another law to deactivate it. In the midst of a prolonged recession, in 2001
doubts began to be cast about the governmentâ€™s ability to curb ï¬scal imbalances. These
fears placed the country risk premium, measured by the diï¬€erence between the interest rate

basis points, among the worldâ€™s highest at the time. Eventually, the Argentine government
defaulted; ï¬rst on interest obligations, in December of 2001, and shortly thereafter on the

heavily upon the underlying monetary policy regime. For example, in the Taylor-rule economy, the expected default rate and the country risk premium are zero. This means that the

economy.

The Perils of Delaying Default: Unpleasant Default
Arithmetics

Thus far, we have considered only two alternative default policies. One is characterized by no
default at any point in time and leads to (ï¬scalist) hyperinï¬‚ation or hyperdeï¬‚ation depending
on initial conditions. The second default policy ensures a path for prices consistent with the
governmentâ€™s inï¬‚ation target and features a stochastic, unforecastable default rate.
But there are indeed inï¬nitely many other possible default arrangements. Here we focus
on one that captures certain aspects of observed pre-default dynamics. Namely, in practice, governments that follow unsustainable policies tend to procrastinate. Only when the
economy is clearly embarked in an explosive path, such as a hyperinï¬‚ation, do governments
dare to make hard decisions, such as defaulting. An example is the pre-default transition
in Argentina in the second half of 2001. A number of observers believed at the time that

described as a rigid peg to the dollar coupled with a precarious ï¬scal stance, default was
simply a matter of time.7 At the end, this sentiments materialized and the Argentine government repudiated its outstanding ï¬nancial obligations. First partially, via a debt swap in
December of 2001, and shortly afterward, in January of 2002, totally. The painful economic
depression and price stampid that followed the default led many to wonder whether the
Argentine government had waited too long to default. When the policy mix is incompatible
with long-run price stability, delaying default may prove counterproductive for two reasons.
