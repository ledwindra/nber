4. Conclusions
This paper provides a preliminary study of the determination of inâ€¡ation and the role of monetary indicators in the euro area, using reconstructed data for 1998â€“2000. A main â€¦nding is
that the P Â¤ model, in which the gap between the current price level and long-run equilibrium
price level is the main determinant of future inâ€¡ation, has substantial empirical support. As in
Svensson [37], given the emphasis on monetary indicators and aggregates, we prefer to express
the P Â¤ model in terms of the real money gap (the gap between the real money stock and the
long-run equilibrium real money stock), which is identical to the negative of the price gap. Thus,
we â€¦nd that the real money gap has substantial predictive power for future inâ€¡ation. It follows
that the real money gap should be an important indicator of future inâ€¡ation for the Eurosystem.
We have also examined the indicator properties for future inâ€¡ation of the output gap and the
Eurosystemâ€™s money-growth indicator (the gap between current M3 growth and the Eurosystemâ€™s
reference value). Surprisingly, the output gap seems to have less predictive power for future
inâ€¡ation than the real money gap. Although the output gap seems to Granger-cause inâ€¡ation,
it does not seem to contain any information not already contained in the real money gap.
Less surprisingly (given earlier theoretical and empirical work), the performance of the Eu17

rosystemâ€™s money-growth indicator is inferior to that of the other two variables. In bivariate
Granger-causality tests, the money-growth indicator does not seem to Granger-cause future
inâ€¡ation. Furthermore, it does not contain any marginal information beyond that already contained in the real money gap and the output gap. To the extent the money-growth indicator
contains any information for future inâ€¡ation, it may be exclusively in its capacity as a proxy
for the real money gap and/or the output gap. Thus, we â€¦nd little empirical support for the
prominent role the Eurosystem has assigned for its money-growth indicator.
Moreover, as argued theoretically in Svensson [34], [35] and [37] and demonstrated empirically
in Rudebusch and Svensson [33], but counter to much conventional wisdom, the lack of predictive
power of the money-growth indicator does not depend on the existence of stable short-run
or long-run money-demand functions. In the present paper, the inferior performance of the
money-growth indicator occurs in spite of well-behaved estimated short-run and long-run moneydemand functions.
In interpreting these results, several caveats need to be kept in mind. First, our empirical
results may be sensitive to the exact choice of data. The way the synthetic euro-area data
has been constructed may matter, and it is possible that other measures of the output gap
are more informative about future inâ€¡ation than the one used here. Second, the real money
gap and the money-growth indicator share with the output gap the dependence on estimates of
potential output, which are notoriously diÂ¢cult and uncertain. Third, calculations of the real
money gap and the money-growth indicator also hinge on the assumed stability of the long-run
income elasticity of money demand, which may shift after the introduction of the euro. The P Â¤
model breaks down without such stability (see Orphanides and Porter [28] and Rudebusch and
Svensson [33]). Of course, the introduction of the euro might be associated with a shift in the
long-run demand for euro which would make the P Â¤ model unreliable, possibly increasing the
relative advantage of the output gap as an indicator for future inâ€¡ation.
Thus, the â€¦rst two caveats above apply in equal measure to all three indicators studied and
do not provide any compelling reason to modify the conclusion that the information content in
the real money gap is superior to that of the output gap and the money-growth indicator. The
third caveat does not apply to the output gap and may hence provide a reason to modify the
conclusions in the direction of increased relative indicator performance for the output gap.
