ABSTRACT
Focusing on tail effects, I incorporate distributions for temperature change and its economic impact
in an analysis of climate change policy. I estimate the fraction of consumption w*(tau) that society
would be willing to sacrifice to ensure that any increase in temperature at a future point is limited to
tau. Using information on the distributions for temperature change and economic impact from studies
assembled by the IPCC and from â€œintegrated assessment modelsâ€ (IAMs), I fit displaced gamma distributions
for these variables. Unlike existing IAMs, I model economic impact as a relationship between temperature
change and the growth rate of GDP as opposed to its level, so that warming has a permanent impact
on future GDP. The fitted distributions for temperature change and economic impact generally yield
values of w*(tau) below 2%, even for small values of tau, unless one assumes extreme parameter values
and/or substantial shifts in the temperature distribution. These results are consistent with moderate
abatement policies.

Introduction.

Economic analyses of climate change policies often focus on a set of â€œlikelyâ€ scenarios â€”
those within a roughly 66 to 90 percent confidence interval â€” for emissions, increases in
temperature, economic impacts, and abatement costs. It is hard to justify the immediate
adoption of a stringent abatement policy given these scenarios and consensus estimates of
discount rates and other relevant parameters.1 I ask whether a stringent policy might be
justified by a cost-benefit analysis that accounts for a full distribution of possible outcomes.
Recent climate science and economic impact studies provide information about less likely
scenarios, and allow one to at least roughly estimate the distributions for temperature change
and its economic impact. I show how these distributions can be incorporated in and affect
conclusions from analyses of climate change policy. As a framework for policy analysis,
I estimate a simple measure of â€œwillingness to payâ€ (WTP): the fraction of consumption
wâˆ— (Ï„ ) that society would be willing to sacrifice, now and throughout the future, to ensure
that any increase in temperature at a specific horizon H, âˆ†TH , is limited to Ï„ . Whether the
reduction in consumption corresponding to a particular wâˆ— (Ï„ ) is sufficient to limit warming
to Ï„ is a separate question which I do not address. Thus I avoid having to make projections
of GHG emissions and atmospheric concentrations, or estimate abatement costs. Instead I
focus directly on uncertainties over temperature change and its economic impact.2
My analysis is based on the current â€œstate of knowledgeâ€ regarding global warming and
its impact. In particular, I use information on the distributions for temperature change from
scientific studies assembled by the IPCC (2007) and information about economic impacts
from recent â€œintegrated assessment modelsâ€ (IAMs) to fit displaced gamma distributions
for these variables. But unlike existing IAMs, I model economic impact as a relationship
between temperature change and the growth rate of GDP as opposed to the level of GDP.

Policy Implications.

The case for any abatement policy will depend as much on the cost of that policy as it does
on the benefits. I do not estimate abatement costs â€” I only estimate WTP as a function
of Ï„ , the abatement-induced limit on any increase in temperature at the horizon H. Clearly
the amount and cost of abatement needed will decrease as Ï„ is made larger, so I consider a
stringent abatement policy to be one for which Ï„ is â€œlow,â€ which I take to be at or below
the expected value of âˆ†T under a business-as-usual (BAU) scenario, i.e., about 3â—¦ C.
I examine whether the fitted displaced gamma distributions for âˆ†T and Î³, along with
â€œreasonableâ€ values of the remaining economic parameters can yield values of wâˆ— (Ï„ ) greater
than 2 or 3% for Ï„ â‰ˆ 3â—¦ C. I also explore â€œtail effectsâ€ by transforming the distributions to
increase skewness or variance while keeping the other moments fixed, and calculating the
resulting change in wâˆ—(Ï„ ). Finally, to explore the case for a stringent abatement policy, I
focus on conservative parameter assumptions, in the sense of leading to a higher WTP.

The IPCC (2007a) surveyed 22 scientific studies of climate sensitivity, the increase in temperature that would result from an anthropomorphic doubling of the atmospheric CO2 e
concentration. Given that a doubling (relative to the pre-industrial level) by the end of the

increase of 7.0â—¦ C or more, and a 1% probability of a temperature increase of 10.0â—¦ C or more.
Thus the 5% and 1% tails of the distribution for âˆ†T clearly represent extreme outcomes;

Putting aside the distributions for âˆ†T and Î³, what are reasonable values for the index of

5.5

Policy Implications.

The policy implications of these results are stark. For temperature and impact distributions
based on the IPCC and â€œconservativeâ€ parameter values (e.g., Î´ = 0, Î· = 2, and g0 =
.02), WTP to prevent any increase in temperature is around 2% or less. And if the policy
objective is to ensure that âˆ†T in 100 years does not exceed its expected value of 3â—¦ C (a
much more feasible objective), WTP is lower still.
There are two reasons for these results. First, there is limited weight in the tails of the
distributions for âˆ†T and Î³. The distribution calibrated for âˆ†T implies a 21% probability
of âˆ†T â‰¥ 4.5â—¦ C in 100 years, and a 5% probability of âˆ†T â‰¥ 7.0â—¦ C, numbers consistent with
the climate sensitivity studies surveyed by the IPCC. Likewise, the calibrated distribution
for Î³ implies a 17% probability of Î³ â‰¥ .00023, also consistent with the IPCC and other
surveys. A realization in which, say, âˆ†T = 4.5â—¦ C and Î³ = .00023 would imply that GDP and
consumption in 100 years would be 5.7 percent lower than with no increase in temperature.20
However, the probability of âˆ†T â‰¥ 4.5â—¦ C and Î³ â‰¥ .00023 is only about 3.6%. An even more
extreme outcome in which âˆ†T = 7â—¦ C (and Î³ = .00023) would imply about a 9 percent loss
of GDP in 100 years, but the probability of an outcome this bad or worse is only 0.9%.
Second, even if Î´ = 0 so that utility is not discounted, the implicit discounting of consumption is significant. The initial consumption discount rate is Ï0 = Î·g0 , which is at least
.03 if Î· = 2. And a (low-probability) 5.7 or 9 percent loss of GDP in 100 years would involve
much smaller losses in earlier years.

GHG abatement policy, they do not imply that no abatement is optimal. For example, 2% of
GDP is in the range of cost estimates for compliance with the Kyoto Protocol.21 Taking the

Conclusions.

I have approached climate policy analysis from the point of view of a simple measure of
â€œwillingness to payâ€: the fraction of consumption wâˆ—(Ï„ ) that society would sacrifice to ensure
that any increase in temperature at a future point is limited to Ï„ . This avoids having to make
projections of GHG emissions and atmospheric concentrations, or estimate abatement costs.
Instead I could focus directly on uncertainties over temperature change and over the economic
impact of higher temperatures. Also, I modeled economic impact as a relationship between
temperature change and the growth rate of GDP as opposed to its level. Using information
on the distributions for temperature change and economic impact from studies assembled
by the IPCC and from recent IAMs (the current â€œstate of knowledgeâ€ regarding warming
and its impact), I fit displaced gamma distributions for âˆ†T and an impact parameter Î³. I
then examined whether â€œreasonableâ€ values for the remaining parameters could yield values
of wâˆ— (Ï„ ) above 2% or 3% for small values of Ï„ , which might support stringent abatement,
and found that for the most part they could not.
For â€œconservativeâ€ parameter values, e.g., Î´ = 0, Î· = 2, and g0 = .015 or .02, WTP to
prevent any increase in temperature is only around 2%, and is well below 2% if the objective
is to keep âˆ†T in 100 years below its expected value of 3â—¦ C. Given what we know about the
distributions for temperature change and its impact, it is difficult to obtain a large WTP
unless Î· is reduced to 1.5 or less, or we assume warming will occur at a more accelerated
rate than the IPCC projects. There are two reasons for these results: limited weight in the
tails of the distributions for âˆ†T and Î³, and the effect of consumption discounting.
It is an understatement to say that caveats are in order. First, although I have incorporated what I believe to be the current consensus on the distributions for temperature
country cost studies surveyed in IPCC (2007c).

The real debate among economists is not so much over the need for some kind of GHG
abatement policy, but rather whether a stringent policy is needed now, or instead abatement
should begin slowly or be delayed for some time. My results are consistent with beginning
slowly. In addition, beginning slowly has other virtues. It is likely to be dynamically efficient
because of discounting (most damages will occur in the distant future) and because of the
likelihood that technological change will reduce the cost of abatement over time. Also, there
is an â€œoption valueâ€ to waiting for more information before adopting a stringent policy that
imposes large sunk costs on consumers. Over the next ten or twenty years we may learn
much more about climate sensitivity, the economic impact of higher temperatures, and the
cost of abatement, in part from ongoing research, and in part from the accumulation of
additional data.
