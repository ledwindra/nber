ABSTRACT
We develop a parsimonious model of innovating firms rich enough to confront firm-level
evidence. It captures the dynamic behavior of individual heterogenous firms, describes the evolution of
an industry with simultaneous entry and exit, and delivers a general equilibrium model of technological
change. While unifying the theoretical analysis of firms, industries, and the aggregate economy, the model
yields insights into empirical work on innovating firms. It accounts for the persistence over time of firms'
R&D investment, the concentration of R&D among incumbent firms, and the link between R&D and
patenting. Furthermore, it explains why R&D as a fraction of revenues is strongly related to firm
productivity yet largely unrelated to firm size or growth.

Endogenous growth theory has sketched the bare bones of an aggregate model of technological change.1 Firm-level studies of R&D, productivity, patenting, and Ô¨Årm growth
could add Ô¨Çesh to these bones.2 So far they have not.
Exploiting Ô¨Årm-level Ô¨Åndings for this purpose raises diÔ¨Écult questions. For instance,

8

Discussion

Having laid out the theory, we ask, what are its implications for Ô¨Årm-level studies of R&D,
productivity, and patenting? What has our analysis contributed to the existing theories
of Ô¨Årm and industry dynamics? Where do we go from here? We conclude by addressing
these questions in turn.

8.1

Interpreting Firm-Level Indicators of Innovation

We have commented along the way when our model Ô¨Åt one of the stylized facts listed in
section 2. Here we return to the questions that motivated our work: Why does R&D vary
so much across Ô¨Årms, and how do these diÔ¨Äerences in research input show up in measures
of innovative output?
8.1.1

R&D Investment

A central prediction of our model is that R&D intensity (R&D as a fraction of sales) is
independent of Ô¨Årm size. While a Ô¨Årm faces diminishing returns to expanding R&D at
a point in time, a larger Ô¨Årm has more knowledge capital to devote to the innovation
process. When R&D investment scales with Ô¨Årm size, as it does when Ô¨Årms choose
R&D optimally, these two eÔ¨Äects exactly oÔ¨Äset each other, leaving both the average and
the marginal productivity of research the same across diÔ¨Äerent sizes of Ô¨Årms. Since the
24

model generates substantial heterogeneity in Ô¨Årm size, it predicts large diÔ¨Äerences in R&D
investment across Ô¨Årms. Since the model generates substantial persistence in Ô¨Årm size, it
also predicts persistence in R&D investment, in line with stylized fact 6.
A second source of diÔ¨Äerences in R&D across Ô¨Årms arises from heterogeneity in research
intensity (as required by stylized fact 4). We posit exogenous permanent diÔ¨Äerences across
Ô¨Årms in the size of the inventive steps embodied in their innovations, with research costs
increasing in the size of the step. Although larger inventive steps are more proÔ¨Åtable,
their increased cost is just enough so that all Ô¨Årms optimally choose to innovate at the
same rate. Nonetheless, Ô¨Årms that take big steps are more research intensive than more
imitative Ô¨Årms taking tiny steps. Since they all innovate at the same rate, the more R&D
intensive Ô¨Årms do not grow faster, and hence end up being no larger on average, than
the more imitative Ô¨Årms. This last result maintains the observed independence of Ô¨Årm
size and R&D intensity (stylized fact 3). Since a Ô¨Årm always takes inventive steps of a
particular size, we capture the persistence of diÔ¨Äerences in R&D intensity (stylized fact
5). Of course, our explanation prompts the question of why a Ô¨Årm is endowed with the
ability to take inventive steps of a particular size. We defer this question for future work.
8.1.2

R&D and Patenting

We can relate the Ô¨Årm-level innovation production function (1) to data under the assumption that each innovation is patented. Our speciÔ¨Åcation of decreasing returns to R&D in
the innovation production function then appears to contradict the observation of constant
returns in Ô¨Årm-level regressions of patents on R&D (stylized fact 2). This contradiction
is only apparent since knowledge capital is also an input in our innovation production
function and R&D is endogenous in the model. In the case of equal sized inventive steps,
the optimal R&D policy implies that Ô¨Årms doing more R&D have more knowledge capital.
Looking across Ô¨Årms that have optimally chosen their R&D investment, the model implies
that patents should follow a Poisson distribution with a parameter proportional to Ô¨Årm
R&D, much like what has been observed.
Allowing for heterogeneous research intensity, but continuing to assume one patent
25

per innovation, the model predicts that patents should rise less than proportionately
with R&D. The reason is that variation in R&D then reÔ¨Çects not only diÔ¨Äerences in
knowledge capital but also heterogeneity in the size of Ô¨Årms‚Äô inventive steps. The expected
number of patents rises in proportion to the former but is unrelated to the latter. If,
however, innovations embodying larger inventive steps are more likely to be patented or
to receive more than one patent, the model could still match the observed constant returns
relationship.
8.1.3

R&D and Productivity

How can we relate a Ô¨Årm‚Äôs productivity to its innovative performance if, as in our model,
the Ô¨Årm innovates by extending its product line? It turns out that while patents are an
indicator of the number of innovations, higher productivity reÔ¨Çects larger inventive steps.
Since a Ô¨Årm‚Äôs R&D intensity is also increasing in the size of its inventive steps, we predict
the observed positive correlation between R&D intensity and productivity (stylized fact
1).
To make this argument precise, consider a Ô¨Årm taking inventive steps of size q. The
step size gives the Ô¨Årm market power which it exploits by setting the prices of its products
equal to a markup q over its constant unit labor cost. Summing across the Ô¨Årm‚Äôs products,
the ratio of its total revenue to its total labor cost is also equal to q. We would typically
measure the Ô¨Årm‚Äôs productivity a as the value of its output divided by employment. Thus
a = qw, where w is the market wage rate. Variation in the size of inventive steps across
Ô¨Årms produces heterogeneity in this measure of productivity. Since we assume that the
size of inventive steps is a characteristic of a Ô¨Årm, we predict persistent diÔ¨Äerences in
productivity.18

8.2

Interpreting Firm and Industry Dynamics

We argued in the introduction that Ô¨Årm innovation and Ô¨Årm growth deserve an integrated
treatment. It turns out that our model of innovating Ô¨Årms has the key elements found in

Our argument about how to interpret measures of Ô¨Årm-level productivity borrows from earlier work
by Klette and Griliches (1996) and Bernard et al. (2000).
18

26

existing models of Ô¨Årm and industry dynamics: heterogeneous Ô¨Årms, simultaneous exit and
entry, optimal investments in expansion, explicit individual Ô¨Årm dynamics, and a steadystate Ô¨Årm size distribution. In contrast to the existing literature, including Simon and
Bonini (1958), Jovanovic (1982), Hopenhayn (1992), Ericson and Pakes (1995), and Sutton
(1998), our model captures all these elements while remaining analytically tractable.
The fundamental source of Ô¨Årm heterogeneity in the model is the luck of the draw in
R&D. A Ô¨Årm grows if it innovates and shrinks if a competitor innovates by improving on
one of the Ô¨Årm‚Äôs products. The Ô¨Årm‚Äôs optimal R&D strategy has it innovate at a rate
proportional to its size. A Ô¨Årm enters if the expected value of a new product covers the
entry cost, and it exits when it loses its last product to a competitor. Together, these
elements of the model capture (i) exit probabilities that are decreasing in Ô¨Årm size (and
age), (ii) Ô¨Årm growth rates that are decreasing in size among surviving small Ô¨Årms, and
(iii) Gibrat‚Äôs law holding as a good approximation for large Ô¨Årms. The dispersion in Ô¨Årm
sizes converges to a stable skewed distribution.
Of course, our model has set aside some important aspects of reality. We assume, as
in Hopenhayn (1992), a continuum of Ô¨Årms and no aggregate shocks. Hence, we have
ruled out aggregate uncertainty as well as strategic investment behavior, features likely to
be important in an industry with just a few competitors. The Ericson and Pakes (1995)
framework is much richer in these respects, but at the cost of substantial complexity.

8.3

Directions for Future Work

Our goal has been to establish a connection between theories of aggregate technological
change and Ô¨Åndings from Ô¨Årm-level studies of innovation. The potential payoÔ¨Ä is twofold.
We have attempted to demonstrate above that our fully articulated equilibrium model
can clarify the interpretation of Ô¨Årm-level empirical Ô¨Åndings. Furthermore, building on
the Ô¨Årm-level stylized facts, the resulting aggregate model is likely to be more credible
both as a description of reality and as a tool for policy analysis.
One direction for future research is to analyze a speciÔ¨Åc industry in which innovation
plays a major role. With Ô¨Årm-level panel data on R&D, patenting, employment, and
27

revenue from such an industry, we could subject the model to a more detailed quantitative
assessment. If it survives such an assessment, the model could help explore diÔ¨Écult
questions about the interactions between industry evolution and technological change.
Another direction is to pursue the model‚Äôs implications for policy. Unlike in its predecessors, in our model incumbent research Ô¨Årms play an important role in driving aggregate
technological change. This feature is essential for evaluating the impact of actual R&D
subsidies, which, as emphasized by MansÔ¨Åeld (1986), are often explicitly designed to act
on the marginal expenditures of R&D-doing Ô¨Årms. We see a potential for extending the
analysis here to address questions that frequently arise concerning policies to promote
innovation.
