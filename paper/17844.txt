ABSTRACT
The paper explores the macroeconomic consequences of fiscal consolidations whose timing and composition
are uncertain. Drawing on the evidence in Alesina and Ardagna (2010), we emphasize whether or
not the fiscal consolidation is driven by tax rises or expenditure cuts. We find that the composition
of the fiscal consolidation, its duration, the monetary policy stance, the level of government debt and
expectations over the likelihood and composition of fiscal consolidations all matter in determining
the extent to which a given consolidation is expansionary and/or successful in stabilizing government
debt.

4 Quantitative Model of Fiscal Consolidation
We now turn to study the macroeconomic consequences of uncertain ï¬scal consolidations in
a richer and more plausible environment. Since debt service costs are particularly important

evidence, the probability of a ï¬scal consolidation in the model rises with the government

consolidation raise marginal costs and inï¬‚ation during the course of the consolidation. Active monetary policy responds to the higher inï¬‚ation by raising real interest rates, which
diminishes the tax base and raises debt service costs. This accounts for the relatively poor
performance of the tax-based consolidations in stabilizing debt when debt levels are high,
even although both tax and spending based consolidations have roughly the same impact on
the primary deï¬cit.
Figure 10 compares the output multiplier under tax- and spending-based consolidations
when the type of consolidation is uncertain, and tax increase and spending cuts in the no-RS
model. At low levels of debt, the two types of no-RS ï¬scal consolidations provide bounds
for the regime-switching model, similar to the comparison with only time uncertainty in

model with composition uncertainty signiï¬cantly outperform the same-sized consolidations
in no-RS model. On the other hand, tax-based consolidations in the model with composition
uncertainty are very similar to tax increases applied in no-RS model. This is due to the expectation spill-over eï¬€ect, as explained in the analytical model in section 3. When economic
agents fear that a consolidation is imminent and are expecting it to be tax-based, they are
relieved to ï¬nd that the consolidation eï¬€ort is spending-based. While the spending cuts do
not lead to an immediate increase in output, they signiï¬cantly reduces their short-run costs
and raises the medium- to long-term beneï¬ts.
If there were never any fear of a consolidation being tax-based, these expectational eï¬€ects
would not apply and the discounted output multiplier from a spending-based consolidation
would always be negative. In contrast, when the realized consolidation is tax-based, but there
was some possibility that it could be spending-based, the output costs of the consolidation

9 Summary
In this paper, we have explored the non-linearities inherent in state-dependent ï¬scal consolidations. The exact timing of these consolidations is uncertain, but the likelihood of observing
a consolidation is rising as debt levels rise. In line with the data, we have contrasted taxbased consolidations with spending-based ones in a variety of contexts, including low- and
high-debt levels, diï¬€erent degrees of monetary policy activism, and diï¬€erent beliefs about future consolidations. Our results show that there are signiï¬cant interactions between all these
factors in determining the marginal impact of a given ï¬scal consolidation. For example, both
tax- and spending-based consolidations can be equally successful in stabilizing government
debt at low debt levels; nevertheless, the response to the same policies can be quite diï¬€erent
at higher debt levels when ï¬scal consolidations are thought to be imminent. In particular,
the monetary policy response to the consolidation and any inï¬‚ation it generates, along with
expectations over the composition of consolidations, are important factors in determining the
outcome. Undertaking a spending-based consolidation is more likely to have an expansionary
eï¬€ect on the economy when economic agents were anticipating a taxed-based consolidation,
especially when monetary policy is actively targeting inï¬‚ation.
In terms of the key policy implications of our analysis, we can draw two main conclusions.
Firstly, the possibility of â€œexpansionaryâ€ ï¬scal consolidations is driven by the resolution
of uncertainty associated with the composition and timing of the eventual consolidations.
Because the possibility of consolidation can act as a drag on economic activity, governments
can enhance economic growth by removing the uncertainty as early as possible, even if doing
so reduces the likelihood of observing an â€œexpansionaryâ€ ï¬scal consolidation subsequently. In
the context of our model, â€œexpansionaryâ€ ï¬scal consolidations are unlikely to be something
that governments seek, but instead reï¬‚ect a failure to rule out growth-reducing policy options
sooner.
Secondly, our analysis also suggests that the inï¬‚ationary consequences of alternative ï¬scal instruments and the monetary policy response to that inï¬‚ation are very important in
determining the outcomes, since debt service costs are a crucial aspect of debt dynamics at
high debt levels. Tax- and spending-based consolidations are fundamentally diï¬€erent in their
23

inï¬‚ation consequences, since distortionary taxation raises marginal costs and fuels inï¬‚ation
in a sticky-price economy, while government spending cuts are typically deï¬‚ationary. These
diï¬€erent inï¬‚ation responses to debt service costs then depend on the monetary policy response to inï¬‚ation. Attempting a ï¬scal consolidation in an environment where interest rates
have reached their zero lower bound, is potentially quite diï¬€erent from doing so when monetary policy is facing an inï¬‚ationary threat. Future research will attempt to assess whether
such factors alter the optimal composition of ï¬scal consolidations, since, for example, the inï¬‚ationary consequences of anticipated tax increases may actually be welcomed in a zero lower
bound environment. More generally, we shall seek to determine the optimal combination of
monetary and ï¬scal policies needed to implement successful ï¬scal consolidations.
