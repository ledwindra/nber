ABSTRACT
Standard measures of productivity display enormous dispersion across farms in Africa. Crop
yields and input intensities appear to vary greatly, seemingly in conflict with a model of efficient
allocation across farms. In this paper, we present a theoretical framework for distinguishing
between measurement error, unobserved heterogeneity, and potential misallocation. Using rich
panel data from farms in Tanzania and Uganda, we estimate our model using a flexible
specification in which we allow for several kinds of measurement error and heterogeneity. We
find that measurement error and heterogeneity together account for a large fraction â€“ as much as
ninety percent -- of the dispersion in measured productivity. In contrast to some previous
estimates, we suggest that the potential for efficiency gains through reallocation of land across
farms and farmers may be relatively modest.
